# CHAPTER ONE

# INTRODUCTION

## 1.1 Background to Study

Surveillance systems are widely used to support security monitoring, access control, incident review, and situational awareness in physical environments such as schools, offices, hostels, laboratories, farms, storage facilities, construction sites, and other sensitive areas. Traditional closed-circuit television (CCTV) systems mainly capture and store video footage for live monitoring or later review. Although this approach is useful for evidence collection, it is largely passive because it depends on human operators to observe camera feeds continuously or inspect recorded footage after an incident has occurred. Manual video monitoring is time-consuming, prone to human error, and becomes less reliable when operators are required to monitor multiple feeds for long periods (Ali et al., 2024; Duong et al., 2023; Elmetwally et al., 2025).

The limitations of passive surveillance have increased interest in intelligent visual surveillance systems. These systems apply computer vision and machine learning techniques to detect objects, identify human presence, recognize abnormal events, and generate alerts when predefined security conditions occur. Instead of only recording footage, intelligent surveillance systems can assist users by drawing attention to events that require response. This is particularly important in environments where security personnel cannot continuously monitor every camera feed.

However, many intelligent surveillance systems are designed under assumptions that may not hold in infrastructure-constrained environments. A number of existing systems depend on stable power supply, high-speed network connectivity, cloud processing, or continuous video streaming. These assumptions are difficult to guarantee in many Nigerian and similar deployment contexts, especially in rural facilities, semi-urban campuses, hostels, farms, construction sites, laboratories, and small warehouses. When connectivity fails, cloud-dependent surveillance systems may be unable to transmit footage or deliver alerts reliably. When power supply is unstable, continuous high-compute processing may also become difficult to sustain. Edge-based surveillance research has shown that cloud offloading and continuous network transmission can introduce latency, bandwidth demand, and deployment limitations, especially when surveillance is expected to operate in real time (Abu Awwad et al., 2024; Ali et al., 2024).

Another important challenge is the computational cost of deep learning-based surveillance. Modern computer vision and video anomaly detection methods can achieve strong performance, but many of them require high processing power, large memory, stable power supply, or graphics processing units. Deep neural networks are often resource-intensive, and complex temporal models for video anomaly detection may be difficult to deploy on energy-constrained devices (Cheng et al., 2018; Duong et al., 2023). Low-cost edge devices such as Raspberry Pi boards have limited computation, memory, storage, and power capacity, making it necessary to balance model accuracy with speed, resource usage, and deployment cost (Ali et al., 2024).

Surveillance performance is also affected by poor lighting and degraded visual conditions. Many security events occur in the evening, at night, or in poorly illuminated spaces. Under such conditions, video frames may suffer from low contrast, noise, blur, weak edge information, and loss of scene detail. These degradations affect both human visibility and machine-based object detection. Studies on low-light image enhancement and low-light object detection show that illumination problems can reduce the effectiveness of detection, tracking, and surveillance analysis (Li et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2018; Qu et al., 2023; Ghari et al., 2024).

Edge computing provides a practical direction for reducing dependence on continuous cloud processing. By performing computation close to the camera, an edge-based surveillance system can process video locally, reduce bandwidth consumption, and continue providing limited functionality even when internet connectivity is poor. Instead of streaming all video frames to a remote server, the system can analyze frames locally and transmit only important events or lightweight alerts. This makes edge computing useful for surveillance scenarios where latency, bandwidth, cost, and connectivity are major concerns (Abu Awwad et al., 2024; Ali et al., 2024).

Edge Sentinel is developed within this context. It is a resilient edge-native intelligent visual surveillance system designed for infrastructure-constrained environments. The system performs local person detection using lightweight computer vision models, applies explainable rule-based security indicators, logs events locally, captures evidence during relevant security events, and delivers alerts through available communication channels. Rather than depending on continuous cloud connectivity, the system is designed to operate locally and degrade gracefully when network or power conditions are poor.

The system does not attempt to solve full general-purpose video anomaly detection. Instead, it uses a lightweight object detection model to identify human presence, then applies deterministic event rules to convert detections into meaningful security events. Examples include restricted-zone presence, lingering, and crowd-density monitoring. This design reduces computational complexity and improves interpretability, which is important for real-world deployment because surveillance decisions should be explainable and verifiable (Tusher et al., 2023; Duong et al., 2023). In this project, privacy is treated as a design consideration, but the primary contribution is resilience: maintaining useful surveillance functionality despite unreliable infrastructure.

## 1.2 Research Motivation

The motivation for this project arises from the need for surveillance systems that remain useful under practical deployment constraints. In many environments, security monitoring is required even when broadband internet is unavailable, power supply is unstable, and continuous human supervision cannot be guaranteed. A camera that only records footage may provide evidence after an incident, but it may not provide timely awareness while the incident is occurring. Similarly, a smart surveillance system that depends heavily on cloud processing may lose much of its value when connectivity becomes poor.

The problem addressed in this study is therefore the limited resilience of conventional and cloud-dependent surveillance systems in infrastructure-constrained environments. Many existing systems are designed around continuous streaming, remote processing, or human monitoring. While such systems may perform well in controlled or well-connected environments, they are less suitable for locations where power, internet, and technical support are unreliable. Some existing edge surveillance studies still assume stable communication infrastructure such as Ethernet or Wi-Fi, even when the models themselves are deployed on edge devices (Batzner et al., 2023; Ali et al., 2024). This creates a gap between model performance and practical deployment resilience.

This project is also motivated by the gap between academic computer vision research and deployment-aware surveillance design. Many studies focus on detection accuracy, anomaly recognition, or model efficiency, but fewer works address the full deployment context, including local event logging, unreliable communication, fallback alerting, and infrastructure limitations. For example, some anomaly detection and crowd analysis approaches report strong model performance but also increase computational load or require further optimization before real-world deployment (Nasir et al., 2024; Abu Awwad et al., 2024). A practical surveillance system must therefore be evaluated not only by model accuracy, but also by its ability to remain operational, notify users, preserve event records, and recover from infrastructure failures.

Edge Sentinel addresses this motivation by combining lightweight local person detection with deterministic behavioural event reasoning and resilient communication. Instead of using a heavy neural model for full behavioural classification, the system uses object detection as the foundation for identifying persons in a scene. Behavioural indicators are then evaluated using rules based on configured zones, time duration, person count, thresholds, and security conditions. This approach is more suitable for Raspberry Pi-class deployment because it reduces computational cost while keeping event decisions understandable.

The project is further motivated by the need for affordable and modular security technology in Nigerian and similar environments. This local deployment context is not heavily represented in the reviewed literature, which makes it part of the practical gap addressed by this work. Schools, hostels, laboratories, farms, storage areas, and small facilities may not be able to rely on expensive enterprise surveillance systems, stable broadband, or continuous cloud subscriptions. A low-cost edge-based system that supports local intelligence, GSM/SMS fallback, and configurable monitoring zones can provide a more practical surveillance option for such environments.

## 1.3 Research Aim and Objectives

The aim of this project is to develop a resilient edge-native intelligent visual surveillance system that performs local human activity detection, generates explainable security events, and delivers alerts through adaptive communication channels in infrastructure-constrained environments.

The specific objectives of the project are to:

1. design an edge-based surveillance architecture capable of performing local person detection and event reasoning without continuous cloud connectivity;

2. implement configurable rule-based security indicators such as restricted-zone presence, lingering, and crowd-density monitoring using lightweight computer vision outputs;

3. develop an adaptive alerting and logging mechanism that supports local event storage, network-based alerts, and GSM/SMS fallback;

4. evaluate the prototype in terms of detection performance, system responsiveness, alert delivery, resource usage, and suitability for infrastructure-constrained deployment.

## 1.4 Methodology

This project adopts an iterative prototype-based software engineering methodology. This methodology is appropriate because Edge Sentinel combines computer vision, embedded hardware, communication modules, event logic, and a user-facing configuration interface. A strictly linear development approach is less suitable because hardware integration, edge-device performance, and model behaviour must be tested and refined through repeated implementation cycles.

The first stage of the methodology involves problem identification and requirements analysis. The project problem was defined around the limitations of surveillance systems in environments with unreliable power, weak internet access, and limited human monitoring. Functional and non-functional requirements were identified, including video capture, local person detection, event processing, local logging, alert delivery, GSM/SMS fallback, configurability, and operation on Raspberry Pi-class hardware.

The second stage involves literature review and technology selection. Relevant literature was reviewed across low-light computer vision, object detection, video anomaly detection, edge computing, and model optimization. The review helped identify the practical limitations of heavy anomaly detection models and cloud-dependent surveillance systems. It also guided the decision to use lightweight object detection and deterministic rule-based event reasoning rather than relying entirely on complex temporal anomaly classification.

The third stage involves system design. The system was designed as a modular edge surveillance architecture consisting of a sensing layer, perception layer, behavioural reasoning layer, event and evidence layer, communication layer, and local interface. The design follows the principle of graceful degradation. When network connectivity is available, the system can deliver richer alerts and support remote access. When connectivity is limited, it continues local detection, stores event records, and uses low-bandwidth alert channels where possible.

The fourth stage involves model selection and benchmarking. The model benchmarking scope is limited to lightweight object detection models suitable for low-cost edge deployment. The selected candidates are YOLOv8n, YOLOv5n, and EfficientDet-D0 / EfficientDet-Lite. These models are considered because they represent practical options for balancing detection accuracy, inference speed, and computational cost. EfficientDet is based on compound scaling for efficient object detection, while YOLO-based models are commonly used in real-time detection tasks and have been applied in edge surveillance contexts (Tan et al., 2020; Abu Awwad et al., 2024; Nasir et al., 2024). Larger YOLO models are outside the scope of the current MVP because they are less suitable for CPU-only edge inference under the project’s resource constraints.

The fifth stage involves prototype implementation. The current prototype is built around a Raspberry Pi 5 and an IMX219 CSI camera, with software modules for frame acquisition, person detection, zone calibration, event generation, local logging, and alert delivery. The MVP focuses on practical, explainable behavioural indicators such as restricted-zone presence, lingering, and crowd-density monitoring. More complex tracking-based or temporal anomaly models are treated as future work unless they become stable within the available implementation period.

The final stage involves testing and evaluation. The system will be evaluated using model-level, system-level, and deployment-level measures. Model-level evaluation may include detection confidence, precision, recall, and mean average precision where labeled test data is available. System-level evaluation includes frame processing rate, end-to-end latency, CPU usage, memory usage, and responsiveness. Deployment-level evaluation includes camera capture reliability, zone calibration behaviour, event logging, alert delivery success, GSM/SMS fallback, and local operation under poor or absent internet connectivity.
